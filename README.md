## What this project does

This platform takes two real-world diabetes datasets and turns them into a fully working ML product вЂ” not just a collection of notebooks. A doctor (or interviewer) can open the Streamlit app, enter a patient's details, and immediately get:

- A **readmission risk score** (0вЂ“100)
- A **disease prediction** with probability
- A **patient risk segment** (Low / Medium / High)
- An **anomaly flag** if lab values look unusual
- **Treatment recommendations** based on similar past patients
- A **SHAP explanation** of why the model made that decision

## Key design decisions

**Why classical ML and not deep learning?**
Tree-based models (XGBoost, Random Forest) consistently outperform neural networks on tabular medical data. They are also faster to train, easier to interpret with SHAP, and require far less data. Deep learning will be added as a future extension.

**Why two datasets?**
The Pima dataset has diagnostic measurements but no treatment history вЂ” you cannot recommend medication from it. The 130-US dataset has medication columns (`insulin`, `metformin` etc.) that make recommendation meaningful.

**Why median imputation for zeros in Pima?**
Columns like `Glucose` and `BMI` use `0` as a placeholder for missing data. We replace zeros with `NaN` then impute with the column median rather than mean because medical measurements are typically skewed.

**Why ROC-AUC over accuracy?**
The Pima dataset is 65/35 imbalanced. A model that always predicts "no diabetes" gets 65% accuracy but is clinically useless. ROC-AUC of 0.50 correctly identifies this as no better than random guessing.
